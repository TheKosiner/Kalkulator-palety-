How many boxes fit on a EUR pallet?

Palletization · approx. 5 min read

This is one of the most common questions in warehouse logistics: how many boxes fit on a EUR pallet? The answer depends on the box dimensions and the maximum allowed load height, but you can work it out in a few seconds. In this guide we show a simple formula, ready-made examples, and the mistakes that most often waste space.

EUR pallet dimensions

A standard Euro pallet (EUR/EPAL) measures 1200 × 800 mm, and the pallet itself is about 144 mm tall. The maximum load height depends on the carrier and the means of transport — most commonly 1800–2200 mm including the pallet is assumed.

Usable area
1200 mm × 800 mm = 960,000 mm² (0.96 m²) per layer.

The formula for the number of boxes

We calculate the number of boxes in two steps — first how many fit in a single layer, then how many layers fit within the height:

  1. Boxes per layer = (1200 ÷ box length) × (800 ÷ box width) — rounding down.
  2. Number of layers = (max. height − 144) ÷ box height — rounding down.
  3. Total = boxes per layer × number of layers.

The key is to check both orientations of the box (lengthwise and crosswise). Sometimes rotating the box 90° lets you fit one more row.

Example: a 400 × 300 × 300 mm box

StepCalculationResult
Per layer(1200÷400) × (800÷300) = 3 × 26 pcs
Layers (max. 1800 mm)(1800−144) ÷ 3005 layers
Total6 × 530 boxes

If we rotated the box (300 × 400), a layer would hold (1200÷300) × (800÷400) = 4 × 2 = 8 pieces — that's 40 boxes instead of 30. That's why it always pays to compare the variants.

The most common mistakes that waste space

  • A single box orientation — skipping the 90° rotation can cost as much as 20–30% of the pallet's capacity.
  • Exceeding the pallet footprint — goods sticking out beyond 1200 × 800 mm are often rejected by carriers and get damaged in transit.
  • Margins that are too large — a few millimeters of slack on each box add up to an entire lost column.
  • No layer interlocking — stacking "in columns" without interlocking reduces stability, even though it gives even counts.
